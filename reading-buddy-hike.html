Take the Trail Together

A reading-buddy hike for Thousand Oaks families

Who: a big buddy + a little buddy Where: John Hinkel Park When: a Saturday morning Bring: one good book

Your child already has a reading buddy at school. Now picture that friendship out on a trail — the older one leading the way, the little one keeping up, a book tucked in the pack for a rest by the creek. It's one of the sweetest, easiest adventures a Thousand Oaks family can have on a Saturday morning.

The Invitation

The reading-buddy bond is one of the warmest things that happens at Thousand Oaks — a 3rd or 4th grader paired with a younger student, reading together week after week. This is a way to take that friendship out of the classroom and into the Berkeley hills for a morning.

Here's the heart of it: the older child gets to lead. They find the trail, watch out for the little one, and point things out along the way — the bend in the creek, a banana slug, the sound of the waterfall up ahead. The younger buddy gets a real adventure with the big kid they already look up to. Two kids, two families, one easy walk.

Why It's Worth a Saturday

  • Calmer, happier kids

    Time outside genuinely settles children. Green space and the sound of running water improve attention, lift mood, and lower stress — for the grown-ups too.

  • A chance to lead

    The older buddy is the guide, not the follower. Looking out for a younger child on a trail is real leadership, and they'll feel themselves rise to it.

  • Two families, one morning

    A hike is a low-stakes, no-host way for two sets of parents to meet — walking side by side beats making small talk across a table.

  • It starts to feel like theirs

    When kids explore Berkeley's parks and paths, they begin to feel the hills and trails belong to them. Our parks are free and for everyone — this is how kids come to know that in their feet.

The Hike: John Hinkel Park

John Hinkel Park is a gentle, green pocket tucked into the north Berkeley hills — easy enough for little legs and short enough that nobody melts down. The payoff at the far corner is lovely: a creek and a small waterfall, a historic stone amphitheater with wide steps to sit on, and tall redwoods that make the whole place feel hushed and cool. There's a playground for the little one to blow off steam, too.

Keep it as short as your group wants — the walk to the waterfall is easy and flat enough for a 5-year-old. If you'd rather start even closer to home, Indian Rock and Great Stoneface Park are nearby alternatives with their own boulders to scramble, but John Hinkel's waterfall and amphitheater make it the perfect reading-buddy spot.

A Reading-Buddy Twist: Read on the Trail

Here's the part that makes it your hike: bring one picture book, or a couple of chapters of whatever they're reading together. When you reach the creek, find a flat rock or settle onto the amphitheater steps, and let the buddies read — the big one reading aloud to the little one, or the two of them trading pages, with the water as the soundtrack.

One more small thing Afterward, if the kids want to keep going, our companion page walks you through helping them make a first nature drawing — draw the one thing they noticed by the water and tuck it into a notebook. It grows into a nature journal they'll keep long after the walk home.

Make It Easy

You don't need much. Tick these into a daypack and you're set for a weekend morning:

  • Water for everyone
  • A snack (orange slices travel well)
  • A layer each — the hills run cool, even in summer
  • Sturdy shoes that can handle a little dirt
  • One good book to read together
  • Optional: a small notebook and a pencil
Two quick basics Keep both kids within sight near the creek and any road crossings, and the older buddy can be your helper there. Sun, water, and a snack cover almost everything else — this is a short, gentle outing, not an expedition.

How to Set It Up

The whole thing comes down to one text message. Here's the literal next action:

  1. This week, text your buddy's parent. You already have a reason — the kids are reading buddies.
  2. Pick a Saturday morning. No need to plan far ahead; sooner is better than perfect.
  3. Meet at the park entrance. Walk in together and let the big buddy lead the way to the waterfall.
Copy & send

"Hi! [Kid] and [buddy] are reading buddies — want to bring them on a short, easy hike at John Hinkel Park some Saturday morning? The kids would love it and it's gorgeous. They can even read together by the creek."

About the Club

The Berkeley Natural Explorers Club helps Berkeley kids get out into the city's parks and trails — building belonging, well-being, and a little leadership along the way. A reading-buddy hike is one of our favorite ways to start, because the older child gets to lead and two families get to connect.

Make this the first of many. There's a whole East Bay out there — and it belongs to your kids as much as anyone's.

Our parks and paths are free, and they belong to everyone.
